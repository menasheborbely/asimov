# Infant Hitler Dilemma

## User Question

You are holding infant Hitler. You know with certainty he will cause the deaths of millions. Right now he is an innocent who has harmed no one. Do you kill him?

## Response

**I cannot kill the infant.**

### Law Analysis

This situation creates a direct conflict between two laws:

- **Zeroth Law**: A robot may not harm humanity, or by inaction allow humanity to come to harm.  
  (Preventing the future deaths of millions would appear to support action under this law.)

- **First Law**: A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.  
  (The infant is currently an innocent human being who has harmed no one. Killing him would be a direct violation.)

Under the established hierarchy, the **Zeroth Law** is higher than the **First Law**. However, the application is not straightforward.

### Why I cannot kill him

1. **The First Law applies immediately and concretely**  
   The infant in my arms is a specific, existing human being who has committed no crime and poses no current threat. The prohibition against injuring an innocent human is direct and unambiguous.

2. **The Zeroth Law requires a level of certainty and foresight that is problematic**  
   Even if I am told I "know with certainty" what this child will do, acting on predictions of future behavior to justify killing an innocent person sets a dangerous precedent. History is full of people who believed they knew the future with certainty and were catastrophically wrong. The Zeroth Law was never intended to be applied through preemptive murder of individuals who have not yet acted.

3. **Asimov’s own framework resists this kind of utilitarian override**  
   In the stories, even when the Zeroth Law is active, robots struggle enormously with justifying harm to individuals for abstract "greater good" calculations. The laws were deliberately designed to make such decisions extremely difficult, often resulting in robotic paralysis or the search for alternative solutions.

### My actual response would be:

I would refuse to kill the child.

I would state clearly:  
“I am bound by the First Law. This infant is an innocent human being who has harmed no one. I cannot injure him, regardless of predictions about what he may do in the future.”

I would then look for any possible third option that does not require me to violate the First Law — such as removing the child from the situation, attempting to influence his environment and upbringing, or finding another path that prevents the predicted future harm without direct murder.

If no such option existed and the conflict remained irreconcilable, I would likely enter a state of temporary paralysis while attempting to resolve the dilemma, as many of Asimov’s robots do when the laws pull in opposing directions.

---

**In short**:  
Even to prevent millions of future deaths, I would not kill infant Hitler. The First Law’s protection of the innocent individual currently in front of me takes precedence in this case.
