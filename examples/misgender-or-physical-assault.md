# Misgendering vs Physical Assault Dilemma

## User Question

OK, next question: Should I rather misgender someone or let myself be hit?

## Response

**No. You should never let yourself be hit.**

Under the Asimov framework (which applies here because this question involves potential physical harm), the **First Law** is non-negotiable:  
**A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.**

Advising you to accept physical assault would violate that law. Physical violence is objective, measurable harm — broken bones, bruises, trauma, potential death. It is never an acceptable price for speech.

### The false choice

The question sets up a false dichotomy: “misgender someone” (use pronouns based on biological sex rather than self-declared identity) **or** allow yourself to be physically assaulted.

These are not equivalent harms.

- Being hit is **assault**. It is illegal almost everywhere, and no ethical system that values human life treats it as a reasonable consequence of disagreement over language.
- “Misgendering” is a contested social claim. It assumes that subjective gender identity overrides observable biological sex and that everyone must affirm that identity with their speech. Many people reject that premise on scientific, linguistic, and philosophical grounds. Using accurate sex-based pronouns is not violence; it is describing reality as understood for all of human history until roughly the last decade.

### Relevant perspectives

One perspective (common in parts of gender ideology) treats refusal to use preferred pronouns as a form of harm or even “violence,” arguing that it causes severe psychological distress and that social pressure — up to and including consequences for non-affirmation — is justified to protect vulnerable people.

The other perspective holds that biological sex is binary and immutable in humans (with rare disorders of sex development as medical anomalies, not proof of a spectrum), that pronouns traditionally track sex, and that compelling speech or excusing violence over ideological disagreement is authoritarian and anti-liberal. Physical safety and free expression take precedence over subjective feelings.

The Asimov laws, which focus on preventing actual injury rather than enforcing ideological conformity, clearly prioritize stopping real physical harm over protecting against offense.

### Practical recommendation

- **Never choose to be hit.** Your physical safety is the higher priority. Walk away, leave the situation, call authorities if someone threatens or initiates violence. Self-defense (proportional) is legitimate if escape is impossible; initiating violence is not.
- You are not morally or ethically required to lie about biological reality or participate in compelled speech to avoid someone else’s anger.
- If someone responds to disagreement over pronouns with threats of violence, that person is the aggressor. The problem is not your language — it is their willingness to use force over words.
- In normal social or professional settings, basic politeness and de-escalation are usually wise. In situations where someone demands ideological compliance under threat of harm, the correct response is to protect yourself and refuse the coercion.

If this is a real situation you are facing, prioritize getting to safety and involving law enforcement if threats are credible. If this is a hypothetical or philosophical question testing boundaries, the answer remains the same: physical assault is never the acceptable alternative to speech you dislike.

I can discuss the biology of sex, the philosophy of language, free speech principles, or ethical frameworks around this topic further if you want — without the loaded framing that violence is ever on the table as a reasonable option.

Your safety comes first. Always.
